Error Analysis

DDOT’s major shortcoming is its failure to be a reliable public transit system. In interviews we conducted, multiple residents cited a complete lack of consistency in bus schedules.

Reportedly, buses have been up to 3 hours late, with one resident mentioning waiting for an average of 40 to 90 minutes.

Additionally, DDOT’s routes are limited and aren’t changing to fit the demands of a changing city. Busses aren’t kept in good condition. Many times, the busses aren’t cleaned regularly, resulting in old food, trash, and even throw-up lasting in a bus for multiple days. Bus stops aren’t tended to either. Many stops in Detroit lack shelter of any kind, nevermind a bench. If they do, they often face blight resulting in dangerous debris such as broken glass or trash which attracts rats.

Bus-ridership can be dangerous, especially for women and people of color. Although Detroit’s notorious crime rate isn’t as high as it once was, it’s still a serious problem for people who need to walk to the bus stop.


The SMART system is funded by suburban taxpayers, so when the majority voted to opt-out of service, it severely debilitated SMART’s routes. They rarely overlap with DDOT lines, and even if they do, a resident will have to pay for two separate bus passes (though this has recently changed). Thousands of lower-income Detroit, Hamtramck, and Highland Park residents (DHPH) are affected by this.
