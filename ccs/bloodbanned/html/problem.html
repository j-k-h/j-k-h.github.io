When I first read the initial article (okay... the article title) I formed my own viewpoint almost instantly as we often do in a liberally opinionated society. I thought: "of course you shouldn't forgo gay sex to give blood" and further "that's unfair. Why would the Red Cross enforce a rule that's so blatantly pointed towards my community?" It was easy, especially then, to feel attacked as a part of a community that has been discriminated against for a very long time.


Every 2 seconds someone in the United States needs blood.
Approximately 36,000 units of red blood cells are needed every day in the U.S.

But as a I continued into the article the complexity of the situation started to reveal itself: the danger of HIV in the gay community, the necessity for blood, the ethics of participating in a society... in the span of a few minutes I went from being certain of the stance I held to being completely lost.


Blood and platelets cannot be manufactured, they must be donated.
Red blood cells must be used within 42 days (or less).

On the one hand I felt that yes, there was discrimination at play here — a year long cooldown felt extreme. But in contrast, it was clear that the red cross had restrictions in place for a reason. Appiah's point that restrictions are often cold and hard also seemed pretty applicable to this situation too. But all-in-all I was pretty unsure. Which is why I thought I’d ask some other people.


Less than 38% of the population is able to give blood or platelets.
Only 7% of people in the U.S. are type O negative meaning it’s always in great demand and often in short supply.
