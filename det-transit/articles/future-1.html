What the Man Wants

Although both of these cities are significantly larger than Detroit, they offer insight into what successful transit systems look like, especially when the government supports them adequately. 


Detroiters commute 91 percent of trips still being completed in private cars. According to Mark de la Vergne, chief of mobility for the city of Detroit, does not believe that an elaborate subway/streetcar system will be the solution. Instead he believes the city should create a more extensive and more frequent bus system through the already existing SMART bus. He plans to accomplish this through fixed route transit.  

“This is not the sexiest answer that a lot of people in this city and region like to hear, but it starts with frequent bus service.”

 “This is not the sexiest answer that a lot of people in this city and region like to hear, but it starts with frequent bus service,” De La Vergne said. He found inspiration from the transit system in Austin, Texas because “lots of people take the bus there”. And also in Seattle, which is noticing “explosive growth,” is said to have an increasing amount of ridership on their bus system as well,  he said everyone takes the bus because it is dependable. 


“Where it starts is frequent bus service,”De La Vergne explains. “You talk about reliability? The bus is gonna be there.” There will still be holes in transit service, but because a bus system takes money to work, until further financial support is provided, there will be less efficient and dependent services. Acknowledging those problems is prioritized by De La Vergne’s team.


“How we fill the gaps so that it’s a mobility system and not just a collection of things is really what we’re trying to explore,” he said.


“If we’re putting down buses that are running every 10 minutes, getting you downtown within 20 minutes, how are we building land use, whether it’s in the city or suburbs?”

De La Vergne later admitted that the audience and panel at the recent M:bility conference in Dearborn likely wasn’t the demographic who would start riding the bus every day.

Instead, he said in part the focus is on new residents that may have moved to Detroit from other cities that have robust public transit riderships.
